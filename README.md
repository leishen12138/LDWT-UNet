## Rethinking Underwater Segmentation: A Physics- and Frequency-Guided Framework
### Abstract
Accurate segmentation of marine animals is fundamentally challenged by the physical properties of underwater imaging, where wavelength-dependent spectral attenuation causes severe channel imbalance and color distortion, and scattering-induced turbidity suppresses high-frequency details while amplifying noise. These effects violate the implicit assumption of information preservation in conventional spatial-domain segmentation models, leading to blurred boundaries and unreliable predictions. To address this issue, we propose LDWT-UNet, a multi-representation learning framework that synergistically integrates physical optical priors and frequency-domain characteristics. Specifically, an Underwater Color Compensation Block (UCCB) approximates the inverse spectral attenuation process via learnable channel-wise modulation to restore color consistency, while a Learnable Discrete Wavelet Transform (LDWT) module decomposes features into frequency subbands for decoupled enhancement of global structures and fine details. A category-guided feature fusion strategy is further introduced to incorporate semantic priors. In addition, we construct the Underwater Animal Perception Dataset (UAPD), which provides fine-grained annotations along with physical degradation metadata and frequency statistics, and establishes a unified Enhancement→Segmentation evaluation protocol. Extensive experiments on UAPD and four public benchmarks demonstrate that LDWT-UNet achieves state-of-the-art performance in mIoU, weighted F-measure, and MAE, while exhibiting superior robustness under challenging underwater conditions.
